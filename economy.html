In-game economy

How money & shop prices work

Endcity uses a player-driven economy: you earn money by playing, trading, and selling—not with real cash. The server shop’s buy and sell prices move over time so busy items don’t stay frozen at one number forever.

The simple version

  • You earn by selling things (to the shop or other players) and doing normal survival stuff.
  • You spend in the shop or by paying other players.
  • Prices change because lots of people buying or selling the same item pushes the numbers up or down—like a busy store running low on stock vs. everyone dumping the same crop.

Still confused? Picture a lemonade stand

If everyone wants lemonade today, the stand might raise the price a little (or pay friends more to bring lemons)—that’s high demand. If everyone keeps bringing lemons and the stand is overflowing, they might lower what they pay for more lemons—that’s high supply.

Our shop does the same idea in the background so one item can’t stay “free money” forever if the whole server farms it nonstop.

For players who want the deeper picture

The shop tracks volume—how many items or blocks moved in a period—and whether most of that flow was players buying from the shop or selling to it. That balance shows up as pressure (demand-heavy vs. supply-heavy). When demand dominates, buy prices and payouts tend to rise until the flow cools. When supply dominates, buy-from-shop prices and sell payouts tend to fall so the economy doesn’t overpay for an oversupplied item.

Nothing here is random: it’s feedback from how the community actually plays. You can still get rich by smart farming and trading—you’re just not competing against a price list that never moves.

Buy price vs. sell price

Buy from shop

What you pay the server to take an item out of stock. If lots of people keep buying the same thing, this number often goes up.

Sell to shop

What the server pays you when you add stock. If everyone floods the shop with the same item, this number often goes down.
